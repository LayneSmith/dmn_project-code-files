Shifting tides of Texas politics

It may not be a blue wave, but recent elections suggest the current may be changing across the state

By Layne Smith | Interactives Journalist

Published Nov. 15, 2018

    Republican U.S. Sen. Ted Cruz’s narrow victory over Beto O’Rourke Nov. 6 could end up being a bellwether for the political leanings of Texas. While the state remains red, Cruz’s margin of victory was only 2.6 percentage points, easily the best a Democratic Senate candidate has done in the state in the last 30 years.

    And while O’Rourke’s near miss left the Democrats without a statewide victory yet again, it does signal that a change may be on the horizon. His campaign furthered gains made by the party in the last four years in some of the state’s largest urban counties, and closed the gap in midsized suburban counties, such as Collin and Denton.

    That’s a good showing for a state party that hasn’t sent a Democrat to the Senate in 30 years or had a candidate carry the state in a presidential election in nearly 40.

    To further study this trend, The Dallas Morning News looked at county-by-county election results for the 2008, 2012 and 2016 presidential races, the 2014 and 2018 Senate races, and the 2010 gubernatorial race. The results illustrate some recent trends:

    A close race The number of Texas counties supporting the Republican candidate, Ted Cruz, outpaced the Democratic candidate, Beto O’Rourke, by a ratio of nearly 7-to-1. However, the small handful of urban counties with the most voters (the larger circles) clearly supported O’Rourke.
    Republican counties The majority of votes for Cruz came from a large number of small, more rural counties, along with some populous suburban counties such as Denton and Collin and suburbs around Houston.
    Democratic counties O’Rourke carried fewer counties, many of which were urban centers with more voters. Cities including Houston, Dallas, Austin and San Antonio showed up for the Democrats in this election.
    Regional records A regional analysis shows certain areas in Texas — the Panhandle, West Texas and East Texas — voted overwhelmingly Republican, while North and South Texas are home to significant Democratic strongholds. Select a region to see how they compare.
    Election history An overlay of every top-ballot race since 2008 highlights both the majority of Texas counties carried by Republicans as well as high-population counties that have been crucial to Democratic campaigns.
    A look at Dallas County Democrats have carried Dallas County in every top-ballot race since 2008 and have pushed the county further blue in the past two elections.
    A look at Dallas suburbs While Dallas County is an established Democratic stronghold, the party has also made up significant ground in the surrounding suburban counties since 2014.
    Tarrant County goes blue Tarrant County, home to Fort Worth, has long been recognized as a Republican stronghold. But O’Rourke was able to flip the county in 2018, continuing a shift to blue that began with the 2016 presidential election.
    Flipping to the Democrats

    Only five Texas counties that voted Republican in the 2008 presidental elecation went for O’Rourke in 2018. Southern Gulf Coast Kenedy County, with its 309 total votes, was the only Democratic county in the 2008 presidential election to go for Cruz in 2018.

    Flipping to the Democrats

    However, 14 Republican counties in the 2014 Senate election turned blue for O’Rourke this year, while no counties went from blue to red over the same period.

    Momentum change

    Voting data shows Democrats made gains in 205 Texas counties since the 2014 election. In many counties, this represents a pullback from a Republican trend leading up to and including the 2014 election. Forty-nine counties trended more Republican from the 2014 Senate race to 2018.

    Texas has been considered a red state for decades. While this recent move toward Democratic candidates may not be a blue wave, data suggests it could be the beginning of a change in the tide.
